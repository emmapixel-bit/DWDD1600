The life of an athlete

The Pressure

Athletes understand pressure. The pressure to make the shot. The pressure to win the game. But there is also the constant pressure to be more, to do more, to achieve more. It is the nature of the beast, and if they are a competitor, which all high-level athletes are, they often accept that challenge and dig in.

Mental and Emotional Toll

This drive for success, however, comes with a toll. Research shows that the mental stress placed on collegiate athletes to continuously achieve more can be overwhelming. A study published in Psychology of Sport and Exercise found that student-athletes experience higher levels of stress than their non-athlete peers, primarily due to the pressure to excel in both their sport and their academics.

Moreover, the pressure to succeed is compounded by the pervasive culture of perfectionism within collegiate athletics. Athletes are frequently expected to not only meet but exceed expectations. In a study from the Journal of Clinical Sport Psychology, athletes reported feeling an intense need to prove themselves, both on the field and in the classroom, and to maintain a high level of performance, which can lead to chronic stress and anxiety. The internal dialogue of “I need to be the best” can contribute to feelings of inadequacy when performance dips or injuries occur.

Beyond academic and performance demands, athletes also face pressures from coaches, teammates, parents, and fans. The public nature of their performance means they are constantly under scrutiny, with every success and failure on display for thousands of spectators. This unique form of social pressure can lead to heightened anxiety, a fear of failure, and in some cases, burnout.

Injuries, a common part of an athlete's life, also contribute significantly to mental health struggles. A sports injury can lead to feelings of isolation, identity loss, and depression, especially if it sidelines the athlete for an extended period. The pressure to return to play quickly can add to the mental burden, creating a cycle of stress and anxiety.

The Schedule

Overall, an athlete’s day may seem to be very simple and strictly tied to routines and schedules, but when looking at a normal day in more detail, the mind is processing surprisingly many factors in order to optimize performance. An athlete's schedule might look something like this:

  1. Wake up at 7:30, have breakfast (whole grain porridge, jam, bread with ham, cheese and tomatoes). The first training session starts at 9:00.
  2. High-intensity workout, long aerobic workout or strength training for 2 hours, having sports drinks at regular intervals.
  3. Followed by a recovery drink, shower, maintenance of sports equipment, and lunch.
  4. An hour to take care of general duties before taking a nap at 15:00.
  5. Then starting the next workout.
  6. The same routines are repeated 7 days a week.

For student-athletes, this rigorous training schedule must be balanced with academic requirements. They often miss classes for games and travel, adding academic stress to their already full plates. Time management becomes a critical skill, as they must balance studies, training, and a limited social life.

Location

Training and Competition Environments

The location of an athlete's life plays a significant role in their training, performance, and overall well-being. This includes a variety of environmental factors, some of which are outside of their control.

  • **The Home Field vs. Travel:** For team sports, the home-field advantage is a well-documented phenomenon. Home teams often perform better due to familiarity with the facilities, the support of the home crowd, and the lack of travel-related fatigue. Traveling teams, on the other hand, face a distinct disadvantage due to schedule disruption, time zone changes, and unfamiliar surroundings.
  • **Playing Surfaces:** The type of surface an athlete plays on can significantly impact their performance and injury risk. For example, some studies suggest that artificial turf can lead to a higher frequency of certain injuries compared to natural grass due to differences in friction and impact absorption.
  • **Environmental Conditions:** Uncontrollable factors like temperature, altitude, and wind can have a big impact on an athlete's body and performance. Competing at a higher altitude, where the partial pressure of oxygen is lower, makes it harder for endurance athletes to achieve optimal performance. Likewise, extreme heat or cold can impair thermoregulation and affect pulmonary function.
  • **High-Performance Facilities:** For elite athletes, the location of their training facility and team resources is crucial. Access to state-of-the-art stadiums, training equipment, and advanced recovery and rehabilitation areas can be a major factor in attracting talent and enhancing performance.
  • **The Olympic Village:** During events like the Olympic Games, athletes reside in a dedicated Olympic Village. This residential complex provides a secure and comfortable environment with state-of-the-art facilities, leisure areas, and accommodation for delegations from around the world.
  • **International Travel and Cultural Adaptation:** International travel exposes athletes to diverse cultures, languages, and lifestyles. While broadening their experiences, it can also present challenges such as adapting to different cuisines, adjusting to unfamiliar social norms, and managing homesickness.
  • **Training Camps and Residency Programs:** Many athletes participate in training camps or residency programs, where they live and train at a specific location for extended periods. These environments offer a focused approach to training, with access to specialized coaching, medical support, and cutting-edge facilities.
