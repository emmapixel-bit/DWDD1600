Who Were the Signers

famous signers

The Declaration of Independence was signed by 56 delegates from the 13 colonies, but a handful of them stand out as especially famous or influential in American history.
John Hancock (Massachusetts)
President of the Continental Congress; famously signed his name in large, bold letters—so “King George could read it without spectacles”—making “John Hancock” a synonym for signature.
Thomas Jefferson (Virginia)
Principal author of the Declaration of Independence; later served as the 3rd President of the United States.
John Adams (Massachusetts)
Driving force for independence in Congress and key defender of the Declaration; later became the 2nd U.S. President.
Benjamin Franklin (Pennsylvania)
The oldest signer at 70; renowned inventor, diplomat, and statesman who helped secure vital French support for the Revolution.
Samuel Adams (Massachusetts)
Leader of colonial resistance and organizer of actions like the Boston Tea Party; influential advocate for independence.
Roger Sherman (Connecticut)
Only person to sign all four great founding documents: the Continental Association, the Declaration, the Articles of Confederation, and the Constitution.
Charles Carroll of Carrollton (Maryland)
The only Catholic signer and one of the wealthiest colonists; outlived all other signers, dying in 1832 at age 95.
Robert Morris (Pennsylvania)
Wealthy merchant known as the “Financier of the Revolution” for funding the Continental Army when congressional funds were scarce.
Richard Henry Lee (Virginia)
Introduced the June 1776 Lee Resolution calling for independence, which set the stage for drafting the Declaration.
George Wythe (Virginia)
Mentor to Thomas Jefferson and the first American law professor; respected legal mind of the era.

Fun Fact: Not all signers signed on July 4th—most actually signed on August 2, 1776, and a few even later.
